# Barrier Taxonomy Justification – AccessLens v2

## Purpose of the Barrier Dataset

The barrier taxonomy in AccessLens v2 defines a structured vocabulary for representing
non-financial obstacles that prevent eligible individuals from successfully accessing
government welfare schemes. This dataset does not describe specific schemes or individuals.
Instead, it captures generic, recurring forms of access friction observed across welfare
delivery systems.

The taxonomy is designed to support simulation and counterfactual analysis by providing
interpretable, reusable barrier definitions that can be applied consistently across multiple
policy domains.

---

## Access Stages

All barriers are mapped to one primary stage of the access journey:

1. **Discovery**  
   The stage at which an individual becomes aware of a scheme and understands that it may
   be relevant to them.

2. **Application**  
   The stage involving form submission, document preparation, and interaction with digital
   portals or physical offices.

3. **Verification**  
   The stage involving checks, approvals, validation by officials or systems, and final
   confirmation of access.

Mapping barriers to stages ensures clarity about *where* access breaks down and allows
stage-specific interventions to be simulated.

---

## Barrier Types

Barriers are grouped into four broad categories, supported by policy literature and
government evaluations:

### 1. Awareness Barriers
These barriers arise when potential beneficiaries are unaware of the existence of a scheme
or lack clarity about eligibility conditions and application procedures. Awareness gaps are
a major contributor to exclusion, especially in decentralized or information-poor settings.

### 2. Documentation Barriers
These barriers relate to the availability, complexity, and verification of required documents.
Even when individuals are eligible, missing or difficult-to-obtain documents can prevent
successful application or approval.

### 3. Digital Barriers
Digital barriers capture limitations related to access to devices, internet connectivity, and
digital literacy. As welfare delivery increasingly relies on online portals, these barriers
have become a significant source of access friction.

### 4. Institutional Barriers
Institutional barriers arise from dependence on intermediaries, discretionary decision-making,
administrative delays, or inconsistent implementation across offices. These barriers often
affect the final stages of access and are difficult for individuals to predict or contest.

---

## Default Severity Values

Each barrier is assigned a default severity value between 0 and 1. These values represent
baseline access friction, not empirical measurements.

Severity values are assigned conservatively using the following logic:
- **0.4–0.5**: Moderate but common friction
- **0.6–0.7**: Strong and frequently observed friction

Values above 0.7 are intentionally avoided in the baseline dataset to preserve headroom for
persona-specific amplification and scheme-specific interactions in later stages of the model.

---

## Ethical and Methodological Notes

- Barrier definitions are generic and do not target any specific population group.
- No personal or sensitive data is used or inferred.
- Severity values are illustrative and explicitly documented as assumptions.
- The taxonomy is intended to support simulation and policy reasoning, not automated
  decision-making or eligibility determination.

This design ensures transparency, interpretability, and ethical use within the AccessLens
framework.
